The Office of the Chief of Staff

Build the chief of staff you can't hire.

Every morning you open five apps just to find out what's on fire. A good chief of staff does that for you — reads the mail, calendar and Slack and, at seven, sends one message: here's what needs your decision today. A lot of that work is now buildable with AI. 01The role

What a chief of staff does — and what AI can take.

Don't start with the tool, start with the work. A human chief of staff does seven things. Some AI already handles well, some still need you, and most work best split down the middle: AI prepares, you decide.

Function Hand to AI Where you stay
What reaches you Of 60 unread emails it surfaces the three that really need you. The rest it files. When an angry key client writes, you reply.
One view, many sources Pulls mail, calendar, Slack and docs into one morning view — instead of five open apps. Reading it and acting on it.
Follow-through Remembers you promised a quote on Tuesday and reminds you Thursday it's still open. When it stalls, escalation and moving deadlines are yours.
Meeting prep Before a client call it preps: what you discussed last time, what you promised, who's on the call. Running the meeting itself.

Decision support For "hire or wait" it lays out the pros and cons and digs up the missing numbers. The decision is yours.
Coordination & politics Watches across teams whether the launch is slipping and flags it early. Reading the mood and the politics.

02Principles

Five things it stands or falls on.

Most attempts at an AI assistant quietly fizzle out. The ones that stick share the same five things. Here they are — as examples, not lectures.

01

One task, not a department

Don't say "run my calendar". Say "every morning tell me which meetings clash". The second one ships; the first falls apart. Most attempts fail because they tried to automate a department, not a task.

02

Give it three of your emails

AI doesn't write like you because it doesn't know you — not because your prompt is wrong. Give it three of your old emails and quality jumps more than any clever wording.

03

Let it come to you

An assistant you have to remember to ask, you use three times and forget. A brief that lands on its own at seven, you read every day. Put the work on a clock.

04

Don't keep a perpetual intern

Without memory you explain who's who and what you're working on every single time. The same system with memory holds it — and after a week it's a useful colleague, not a first-day intern.

05

Let it just suggest first

Don't hand the agent the right to send emails for you on day one. The first week, let it only suggest and you click "send". What it does reliably, then let it run.

03How it matures

Four levels, from answering to acting.

You don't leap straight to an autonomous operator. Each level adds one thing and earns the next. Know where you are — and what the next step needs.

L1Reactive

The assistant on call

You paste a 40-page report and say "summarize this". A minute later you have a summary. Great — but nothing happens until you ask.

L2Scheduled

The standing brief

At seven, a single view lands on its own: two meetings clash, three emails await a reply, one task is overdue. You haven't lifted a finger. This is where it starts to pay off.

L3Memory

The system that remembers

It knows "Sarah" is from Nordia and what you discussed last time. Correct it once ("send this straight to Tom") and it does it itself next time. You stop explaining over and over.

L4Autonomy

The operating system

One agent triages the inbox, another watches the calendar, a third preps for meetings — and something above them runs the show. Anything irreversible (send, delete) you still approve. At this level you don't run it; it runs the office. Mature real-world systems work exactly like this.

04How to build it

Four phases, each with something done.

No grand architecture. Four steps, each ending with something that actually runs and that you can measure. Don't start the next phase until the previous one works.

Phase 0 — Choose

Pick one painful thing

Take what annoys you most — say the morning inbox triage. Measure what it costs you today: say 45 minutes. That's your number. Now resist adding more.

# scope.md One thing: morning inbox triage Today: 45 min/day Done when: 5 emails to act on each morning, the rest tagged and archived
Phase 1 — Onboard

Brief it like a new hire

Don't write commands, give context. Five short files: who you are, how you write (attach a few of your emails), what to avoid, what you're working on now, how to work with you. Twenty minutes that pay back for months.

# context/ who-i-am.md how-i-write.md what-to-avoid.md priorities.md rules.md
Phase 2 — Remember

Memory and the daily brief

Give it a notebook it reads and writes (a notes folder is fine). And ship the first brief: decisions up top — what, who, by when. Anything unfinished carries to tomorrow so it doesn't get lost.

# brief every morning DECIDE → who · by when TRACK → what's open + how long CARRY → from yesterday
Phase 3 — Run

Schedule, learn, watch

Run it on a clock (and on things like an incoming email from the boss). When it gets something wrong, tell it — and have it remember. Keep sending and deleting behind approval. What it handles without errors, let it run on its own.

# the loop clock + events correction → rule irreversible = human approves

05Proven parts

The moving parts — and how the good ones fit.

Patterns from systems that hold up in production. Borrow them; you don't have to invent the architecture.

Integration

Three layers in

Gmail connects in a few clicks. For deeper things there's MCP. And whatever has no API — like WhatsApp — you reach through the browser. Most people need all three.

Triggers

Clock plus events

The brief runs in the morning on a clock. But when an email from your biggest client arrives, you want to know now — that's an event. Combine both.

Oversight

Approval in steps

The first week, approve every email it sends. Once you see it can do it, spot-check every tenth. Eventually only exceptions. The bigger the risk, the more oversight.

Memory

Verified knowledge base

For every fact, show where it's from: confirmed by two sources, single source, unverified, stale. Never let it act on the soft ones alone.

Trust

A record of everything

Have it version every change it makes (Git is fine). When it does something odd, you check the history and know exactly what and when. Without that you'll never trust it.

Cost

Budget as a safety net

Set a cap per day and per run. Go over and the run is simply skipped. No surprise on the invoice.

06Governance & trust

The part the C-suite can't skip.

Your inbox carries acquisitions, deals and people decisions. The system that touches them needs guardrails before features. Six not to forget.

1

Keep the sensitive apart

The agent reading acquisition emails has no business in the same inbox as the one booking your lunch.

2

Only what's needed

An inbox-triage assistant doesn't need access to accounting. Grant only what the job truly requires.

3

Secrets aside

Passwords and keys should be filled in only at the moment they're needed. Never sitting in the text the model sees.

4

Its own environment

Don't run an agent that operates on its own on your work laptop. Give it its own machine or a sandbox.

5

Keep a record

For every important action, make clear who approved it. What AI does for you, someone may review later.

6

Choose hosting on purpose

Keep sensitive things in-house (self-host or in-tenant). Where the data allows, use a ready service for speed.

08Where it goes wrong

Why most attempts fail — and what to do instead.

Published estimates put failure rates for AI agent projects between 70 and 95 percent. The reasons rhyme. Here's each one as an example, paired with the fix.

✕You say "handle all my admin".
→Shrink it to one task with a clear "done". Like: "triage my inbox and flag what's urgent".
✕You automate a process no one ever wrote down.
→Walk through it once and write it down first. AI just exposes the mess; it doesn't tidy it.
✕It worked in the demo, breaks on real data.
→Test on your real, ugly emails — not a tidy example.
✕One step fails silently and breaks the chain.
→Design for retries, and make irreversible steps (send, delete) need a human.
✕It makes up a fact and sounds confident.
→Have it answer from traceable sources and admit what's uncertain.
✕It keeps making the same mistake.
→Build the learn-from-corrections loop on day one. Otherwise it repeats it forever.
